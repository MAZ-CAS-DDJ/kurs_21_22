Goals\n>>\n>> *By ABHIJIT BANERJEE and VARAD PANDE*\n>>\n>> SEPT. 10, 2014\n>>\n>> Cambridge, Mass. \xe2\x80\x94 In France, children grow up hearing the story of the\n>> 100 times good cake: A cat and a dog preparing a cake start from the idea\n>> that if the cake has 100 delicious ingredients, it will be \xe2\x80\x9c100 times\n>> good.\xe2\x80\x9d So they make a cake with strawberries and cream, garlic and pepper\n>> (and throw in a mouse and some bones for good measure) \xe2\x80\x94 with predictable\n>> consequences.\n>>\n>> Diplomats are facing a \xe2\x80\x9c100 times good\xe2\x80\x9d temptation as they work to\n>> establish new United Nations\n>> <http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/u/united_nations/index.html?inline=nyt-org> global\n>> objectives for development, known as Sustainable Development Goals, that\n>> will help set an overarching narrative for the world\xe2\x80\x99s progress for the\n>> next 15 years.\n>>\n>> The diplomats from 70 countries made up an Open Working Group, which\n>> recently submitted its proposal. A diplomat from a small Pacific island\n>> that faces imminent inundation might, understandably, have focused on the\n>> elimination of fossil-fuel subsidies, while one from China or India might\n>> have stressed the transfer of environmentally sound technologies to\n>> developing countries on favorable terms. By themselves, these are both\n>> worthy causes, but a result of accommodating these divergent priorities is\n>> a list with 17 goals and 169 targets as metrics for measuring progress\n>> toward those goals \xe2\x80\x94 a sort of \xe2\x80\x9c169 times good cake.\xe2\x80\x9d\n>>\n>> The previous Millennium Development Goals\n>> <http://mdgs.un.org/unsd/mdg/host.aspx?Content=indicators/officiallist.htm>,\n>> established in 2000 with a target date of 2015, set only eight broad goals\n>> \xe2\x80\x94 like universal primary education, gender equality and environmental\n>> sustainability \xe2\x80\x94 as priorities for global resources, and just 19 targets.\n>>\n>> The power of the original millennium goals came from their very clear\n>> prioritization of a small number of measurable objectives. The idea was to\n>> present to the world a specific vision that said, \xe2\x80\x9cThis much at least we\n>> should be able to offer every human being.\xe2\x80\x9d By emphasizing the sheer\n>> modesty of what was being proposed, it made it hard for nation states to\n>> ignore the global project.\n>>\n>> What is needed now is a clear, concise set of objectives. Without them,\n>> the entire project is in very real danger of failing. If nations can simply\n>> ignore the imperatives on the grounds that they are too many, too grandiose\n>> and too far out of touch with countries\xe2\x80\x99 limited resources and ability to\n>> effect change, the development goals will just be another pious hope in the\n>> long list of United Nations-sponsored fantasies.\n>>\n>> We have some experience of just how difficult choosing priorities can be.\n>> We were both involved in the High-Level Panel for the Post-2015 Development\n>> Agenda, a group set up by the secretary general that worked in parallel to\n>> the Open Working Group. We submitted our report\n>> <http://www.post2015hlp.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/05/UN-Report.pdf> \xe2\x80\x94\n>> which will be combined with the Open Working Group\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x94 in May last year,\n>> and despite our attempts to discipline ourselves ruthlessly, we ended up\n>> with 12 goals and 54 targets.\n>>\n>> Choices have to be made. Some are easier than others: For example,\n>> \xe2\x80\x9cDevise and implement policies to promote sustainable tourism\xe2\x80\x9d (Target 8.9\n>> in the diplomats\xe2\x80\x99 proposal), laudable as it is as an objective, cannot\n>> possibly lay the same claim to our attention as infant mortality\n>> <http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/i/infant_mortality/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier> or\n>> mass illiteracy.\n>>\n>> The list of targets could also be shortened by focusing on outcomes and\n>> leaving out process or input measures (the current version has both). This\n>> has the added advantage of allowing countries to use their limited\n>> resources as they see fit. For example, we think it is much better to have\n>> a quantitative target for children\xe2\x80\x99s learning (e.g., by 2030, X percent of\n>> children should be reading or doing math at their grade level) than to\n>> require them to \xe2\x80\x9cincrease by X percent the supply of qualified teachers\xe2\x80\x9d\n>> (Target 4.c in the working group proposal), especially given the lack of\n>> evidence that teacher training as currently delivered has much effect on\n>> children\xe2\x80\x99s learning.\n>>\n>> Moreover, the goals and targets should be as specific, measurable and\n>> actionable as possible. For example, Target 12.2 in the proposal asks\n>> countries to \xe2\x80\x9cby 2030 achieve sustainable management and efficient use of\n>> natural resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Who would argue with that, but what does it actually\n>> require countries to do other than to say amen?\n>>\n>> Finally, our report indicated some issues that apply to many goals.\n>> Inequality was one. The idea was that for a range of goals, countries would\n>> have to measure and report the outcomes \xe2\x80\x94 for example, infant mortality \xe2\x80\x94\n>> for the poorest X percent (say, 20 percent) of the population, in addition\n>> to the average.\n>>\n>> This is where we find a lot of the historically disadvantaged populations\n>> (the Roma in Europe, \xe2\x80\x9cscheduled tribes,\xe2\x80\x9d as the indigenous people in India\n>> are known, African-Americans) and help bring some attention to\n>> subpopulations without focusing on ethnicity directly.\n>>\n>> It also makes it harder for countries to concentrate just on people close\n>> to the poverty line and ignore those far below and points to overlooked\n>> communities in the richest countries.\n>>\n>> The United Nations General Assembly has its work cut out. It must balance\n>> ambition with practicality. It must devise a tight agenda for the world to\n>> collectively strive toward \xe2\x80\x94 and remember that more ingredients do not\n>> always make the best cake.\n>>\n>> *Abhijit Banerjee  <http://economics.mit.edu/faculty/banerjee>is the\n>> international professor of economics at M.I.T.
