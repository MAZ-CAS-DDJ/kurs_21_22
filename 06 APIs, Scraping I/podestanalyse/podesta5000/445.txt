Ukraine seems to be heading toward an informal settlement largely on European and U.S. terms. Yet this success could threaten the very Western unity that brought it about.\n  \nOver the past three months, Ukraine has witnessed a stable cease fire, partial withdrawal of military equipment, scheduling of federal instead of separatist elections, and a plan to bolster OSCE monitoring. Russian President Vladimir Putin seems resigned to pursue his aims primarily through economic and political means. In hard-headed Kissingerian terms, two years of conflict have shifted Ukraine from the Russian sphere of influence into the Western camp. Putin\xe2\x80\x99s actions in Crimea have failed to undermine the post-Cold War global order elsewhere in the world.\n  \nThis marks a considerable Western achievement. Of course the result is not perfect. Crimea remains \xe2\x80\x94 and, realistically, will remain \xe2\x80\x94 Russian. Ukraine continues to be economically vulnerable. Complex negotiations will be required to unfreeze the conflict in its eastern provinces \xe2\x80\x94 and they may fail. For the moment, however, facing a no-win situation, Putin is playing along.\n  \nYet over the next six months, transatlantic and intra-European tensions may distract Western governments from the successful formula they have pursued, which stresses civilian power wielded by a German-led Europe.
